Lesson 0002 · ~60 minutes · Phase 1

Measure Your Machine's Roofline

The one thing this lesson teaches: any kernel or model can be placed on a single chart — the roofline — that tells you whether it's compute-bound or memory-bound, and therefore which optimizations can possibly help. You'll build that chart for your M4 Max from your own measurements.

1 · The idea in three sentences

Every operation does some math (FLOPs) and moves some bytes. The ratio is its arithmetic intensity: AI = FLOPs / bytes moved. Your machine has two ceilings — peak compute (FLOP/s) and peak memory bandwidth (bytes/s) — and whichever one an operation hits first is its speed limit (Horace He calls these compute-bound vs bandwidth-bound regimes).

The crossover point is the ridge point:

ridge (FLOPs/byte) = peak compute (FLOP/s) ÷ peak bandwidth (B/s)

AI below the ridge  → memory-bound  → more TFLOPs won't help
AI above the ridge  → compute-bound → more bandwidth won't help

2 · Measure your two ceilings

Spec sheets lie (or at least round up). Inference engineers measure. Save this as roofline.py in your workspace and run it in the venv from lesson 0001:

Step 1 — The benchmark script

import time
import mlx.core as mx

def bench(f, iters=20, warmup=5):
    for _ in range(warmup):
        mx.eval(f())
    t0 = time.perf_counter()
    for _ in range(iters):
        mx.eval(f())
    return (time.perf_counter() - t0) / iters

# --- Ceiling 1: compute (big fp16 matmul, the friendliest workload) ---
N = 4096
A = mx.random.normal((N, N)).astype(mx.float16)
B = mx.random.normal((N, N)).astype(mx.float16)
t = bench(lambda: A @ B)
tflops = (2 * N**3) / t / 1e12
print(f"matmul {N}x{N}:  {t*1e3:.2f} ms   ->  {tflops:.1f} TFLOP/s (fp16)")

# --- Ceiling 2: bandwidth (elementwise add: read 2 arrays, write 1) ---
M = 256_000_000                      # 1 GB per fp32 array
X = mx.random.normal((M,))
Y = mx.random.normal((M,))
t = bench(lambda: X + Y)
gbps = (3 * M * 4) / t / 1e9
print(f"elementwise add: {t*1e3:.2f} ms   ->  {gbps:.0f} GB/s")

print(f"ridge point: {tflops*1e12 / (gbps*1e9):.0f} FLOPs/byte")

Why these two workloads: a large matmul does 2N³ FLOPs while moving only ~3·2N² bytes (AI ≈ 1,365 — deep in compute-bound territory), so it exposes the compute ceiling. Elementwise add does 1 FLOP per 12 bytes (AI ≈ 0.08) — pure memory traffic, so it exposes the bandwidth ceiling.

Predict before running: (a) Will measured bandwidth land above or below the marketing number for your machine (546 GB/s)? (b) Will the ridge point be closer to 5, 50, or 500 FLOPs/byte?
Typical results & what they mean

Expect bandwidth around 350–480 GB/s (achievable is always below nominal — page faults, cache effects, write-allocate traffic) and fp16 matmul somewhere in the tens of TFLOP/s. The ridge point usually lands in the tens of FLOPs/byte. The exact values don't matter; your values do — write them down, they're constants you'll reuse for months. If a number looks absurd (e.g. 5 GB/s), ask your teaching agent before moving on — first-run compilation or unified-memory paging may have polluted the timing.

3 · Place last lesson's workloads on your roofline

Arithmetic intensity of decode vs prefill

In decode, each token is roughly a series of matrix–vector products: every weight is read once and used for ~2 FLOPs.

WorkloadAI (FLOPs per byte of weights)vs your ridge
Decode, fp16 weights2 FLOPs / 2 B = 1far below → memory-bound
Decode, 4-bit weights2 FLOPs / 0.5 B = 4still below → memory-bound
Prefill (long prompt) / big matmulhundreds–thousandsabove → compute-bound

This is lesson 0001's asymmetry, now expressed precisely: decode sits at AI ≈ 1–4 against a ridge in the tens. No amount of compute fixes that — only moving fewer bytes (quantization, smaller models) or sharing each byte across more work (batching).

4 · The punchline: batching moves you along the roofline

If 8 requests decode together, each weight byte read from memory now serves 8 matvecs: AI multiplies by the batch size. Somewhere around batch ≈ ridge ÷ AI_single, decode crosses the ridge and becomes compute-bound. This single observation is why continuous batching (vLLM, month 5) exists, and why serving economics reward batch throughput.

5 · Self-check

  1. Using your measured ridge point: roughly what batch size makes fp16 decode compute-bound?
  2. A vendor offers you a chip with 4× the TFLOPs and the same bandwidth. How much faster is single-stream decode?
  3. Why did we benchmark with mx.eval() inside the loop?
  4. From lesson 0001: explain your 4-bit vs 8-bit result in roofline vocabulary, in one sentence.
Answers
  1. batch ≈ ridge ÷ 1. If your ridge is ~40 FLOPs/byte, around batch 40 for fp16 (≈ ridge ÷ 4 for 4-bit weights).
  2. Essentially no faster (~1×). Decode at AI ≈ 1 is bandwidth-bound; the extra TFLOPs sit idle.
  3. MLX is lazy — without forcing evaluation you'd time graph construction, not execution. (Same trap exists in CUDA with async launches; you'll meet cudaDeviceSynchronize in month 2.)
  4. Halving bytes per weight doubled arithmetic intensity, and since decode is memory-bound, throughput scaled with bytes moved — ~2× faster.

6 · Session follow-up: what if TFLOPs doubled?

After measuring 14.2 TFLOP/s and 436 GB/s (ridge ≈ 33 FLOPs/byte), this retrieval question came up in session:

Q: Same bandwidth, double the TFLOPs (28.4). What happens to the ridge point — and does your local LLM generate any faster?

Answer given: "Bandwidth, not compute, sets the ceiling — if we double the TFLOPs the machine will sit idle."

Verdict: Right where it matters most — decode tok/s doesn't improve at all; compute just idles harder. Two refinements: (1) the ridge doubles to 28.4 ÷ 0.436 ≈ 65 FLOPs/byte, meaning more workloads now count as memory-bound and the fp16 batch crossover moves from ~33 to ~65; (2) "sits idle" is true for decode, but prefill is compute-bound, so time-to-first-token would roughly halve. One hardware change, two different per-phase verdicts.

Your roofline, before and after doubling compute

How to read it: workloads live on the chart at their arithmetic intensity; their speed limit is the lower of the two ceilings above them. Doubling compute lifts only the flat roof — and only workloads to the right of the ridge ever touch it. Your decode points sit far left on the unchanged green bandwidth slope, which is why generation tok/s doesn't move. Prefill sits on the flat roof, so it rises with it. And the ridge sliding right (33 → 65) means batching now has to work twice as hard before decode goes compute-bound.

What "doubling TFLOPs" means physically

TFLOP/s is set by how much math silicon you have and how fast it cycles. Hardware designers get there four ways:

  • More compute units — more GPU cores (NVIDIA: more SMs; Apple: M4 Pro's 20-core GPU vs your M4 Max's 40-core). Literally more square millimeters of ALUs.
  • Higher clocks — the same ALUs cycle more often. Costs power and heat quadratically-ish, so it never gives 2× alone.
  • Wider math per unit — dedicated matrix engines that do many multiply-accumulates per cycle (NVIDIA tensor cores; Apple's neural accelerators). The cheapest big multiplier in modern chips.
  • Lower precision — fp16 → fp8 doubles rated TFLOPs on the same transistors. Spec sheets love this; always check which precision a TFLOPs claim refers to.

Bandwidth lives in a different part of the chip: memory controllers, bus width (your M4 Max pairs its 40 GPU cores with a 512-bit LPDDR5X bus — that's where 546 GB/s comes from; the M4 Pro's narrower bus gives 273 GB/s), and DRAM speed. Because compute and bandwidth come from separate silicon budgets — and ALUs are cheaper to add than memory pins — every GPU generation raises the ridge: compute grows faster than bandwidth. Each generation, more of the world becomes memory-bound. That trend is, in one sentence, why inference engineering is mostly memory engineering, and why this lesson's chart is the one you'll keep redrawing for years.

Your win: you now own two measured constants for your machine (peak TFLOP/s, peak GB/s) and one derived one (ridge point), and you can classify any workload as compute- or memory-bound before optimizing it. Interviewers probe exactly this; profilers like Nsight Compute (month 3) just automate the chart you built by hand today.
